Module 1: Stress and the Nervous System

This module focuses on how stress affects the human body, particularly the nervous system.
It delves into the different levels of the brain: the vegetative level, the limbic system, and the neocortical level.
The reticular activating system (RAS) is discussed in detail, emphasizing its role in regulating arousal and attention.
The module also covers the brain stem and its functions, along with the importance of the autonomic nervous system (ANS).
The ANS comprises the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems, responsible for the fight-or-flight response and relaxation, respectively.

Module 2: Stress and the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis

The endocrine system's crucial role in stress response is examined.
The focus is on the HPA axis, a network of glands that release hormones during stress.
This module explains how the hypothalamus, pituitary gland, and adrenal glands work together to activate the HPA axis.
It highlights the role of cortisol and epinephrine in the stress response and their effects on the body.
The module also discusses the concept of allostatic load, the negative consequences of prolonged stress, and the importance of maintaining cortisol balance for overall health.

Module 3: Stress and the Immune System

This module focuses on the intricate connection between stress and the immune system.
It explores the immune system's role in defending the body against pathogens and how stress can suppress its functions.
The module delves into the history of research on stress and the immune system, highlighting the work of Janice Kiecolt-Glaser and Ronald Glaser.
It examines the concept of allostatic load and its impact on immune function, emphasizing the role of cortisol in both protecting and harming the immune system.
It discusses the consequences of both elevated and lowered cortisol levels and how these affect the body's ability to fight off infections.
The module concludes with strategies to deal with stress-induced immune system suppression, including building social support and practicing relaxation techniques.

Module 4: Health Risk Associated with Chronic Stress

The focus of this module is on the harmful effects of chronic stress on various body systems.
It examines the impact of stress on the musculoskeletal, cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, respiratory, and endocrine.

Stress Management

Module 1: Developing Cognitive Coping Skills

Coping: The thoughts and actions we use to deal with a threatening situation.
Coping Skills (Strategies): Defense mechanisms to avoid or lessen psychological pain.
Maladaptive Coping Skills: Ways of dealing with stress that usually make things worse.
Building Resilience to Stress:
Prevention: Decreasing the source of stress and increasing coping skills.
Targeted Intervention: Helping the individual change the source of stress and teaching ways to adapt to situations without stress.
Coping Styles:
Problem-solving: Focuses on ways to tackle the issue.
Emotion-focused: Gathers tools to nurture one's emotional health.
Active Coping: Involves awareness of the stressor and attempts to reduce negative outcomes.
Avoidant Coping: Ignores the issue, often resulting in activities that aid in the denial of the problem.
Specific Coping Strategies:
Humor: Pointing out the amusing aspects of the problem.
Seeking Support: Asking for help or emotional support.
Problem-solving: Locating the source of the problem and determining solutions.
Relaxation: Engaging in relaxing activities or practicing calming techniques.
Physical Recreation: Regular exercise, such as running or team sports.
Adjusting Expectations: Anticipating various outcomes.
Denial: Avoiding the issue altogether.
Self-blame: Internalizing the issue and blaming oneself.
Venting: Expressing emotions to friends or family.

Module 2: Other Relaxation Techniques

Biofeedback: Uses electronic instruments to monitor and physiological changes.
Diaphragmatic Breathing: Deep, slow breathing.
Yoga and Stretching: Various types of yoga, including Hatha yoga, which includes stretching.
Mindfulness: Focusing attention on the present moment.
Tai Chi: Gentle physical exercise and stretching, originally formed for self-defense.

Module 3 - Do It Yourself Strategies for Stress Management

Self Help: Have a positive attitude to help yourself to bring about a change.
Identify stressors:
Identify vulnerable zones of your body (aches, pains, appetite, digestion, sleep).
Identify disturbing Negative Automatic Thoughts (NAT) triggered during stress.
Chart down strategies you have used so far to deal with them - adaptive or maladaptive?
Explore strategies to avoid, minimize, modify, or modulate stressors.
Employ strategies to reduce excessive arousal and dysfunction:
Remind yourself of the relationship between thought, mood, and behavior.
Thoughts: Awareness of NAT, employing strategies to stop them.
Behavior: Exploring opportunities, facing situations rather than avoidance, engaging in face-to-face social interactions, and employing problem-solving strategies.
Strategies to ventilate or express the stress response:
Practicing relaxation techniques.
Regular exercise.
Practicing assertiveness.
Time management techniques.
Time Management:
Time awareness and time scheduling:
Prepare an activity chart.
Get a planner.
Evaluate yourself for over/underestimation of time.
Task: Plan two activities, write down expected and actual times, and evaluate the task.
To-do list:
Break problems into small parts.
Prioritize tasks.
Set a time to write down the next day's agenda.
Stick to commitments.
Evaluate at the end of the day.
Reward yourself.
